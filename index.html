Artifact 1 · Presentation

A Brief and Slightly Embarrassing History of AI

This artifact is a timeline-based presentation that explains the development of artificial intelligence from early ideas like the Turing Test to modern tools such as ChatGPT and multimodal AI systems.

Objective: To understand the major historical milestones that shaped artificial intelligence and connect them to today’s AI/ML landscape.

Process: I organized important AI events into a chronological timeline, including the Turing Test, Dartmouth Conference, AI winters, expert systems, Deep Blue, AlexNet, AlphaGo, transformers, and ChatGPT.

Tools and Technologies Used: Microsoft PowerPoint, AI history research, timeline design, visual storytelling.

Value Proposition: This artifact demonstrates my ability to explain the evolution of a complex technical field in a way that is clear, organized, and engaging.

Unique Value: The presentation uses humor and storytelling to make AI history easier to understand for students and beginner audiences.

Relevance: Understanding AI history helps explain why today’s AI systems exist and how past successes and failures shaped modern AI development.

Artifact 2 · Research Report

STORM Research Report: The Pros and Cons of Remote Work

This artifact is a research-style report created with STORM on the pros and cons of remote work. The report explains benefits such as flexibility, productivity, cost savings, and accessibility, while also discussing challenges such as isolation, communication barriers, cybersecurity risks, and work-life balance concerns.

Objective: To explore how an AI research assistant can generate a structured report on a real-world workplace topic and evaluate the quality of the final output.

Process: I used STORM to research the topic of remote work. STORM generated search queries, searched for sources, ranked relevant results, used semantic matching, extracted useful passages, summarized information, and organized the material into a report.

Tools and Technologies Used: STORM, ChatGPT, Claude, AI-generated research workflows, PDF report review.

Value Proposition: This artifact demonstrates my ability to use AI tools for research, evaluate AI-generated content, and identify where the output is helpful versus where it needs deeper analysis.

Unique Value: I did not only accept the report as complete. I compared how different AI tools responded to related questions. ChatGPT explained GPT concepts in a beginner-friendly way, while Claude provided a more technical and visually appealing explanation. I also asked ChatGPT for color and design recommendations to improve the portfolio presentation.

Relevance: This artifact connects directly to foundational AI learning because it shows how AI tools can support research, writing, summarization, and content organization while still requiring human judgment and evaluation.

Critical Evaluation: The final STORM report was clear and easy to follow, but it was also somewhat surface-level. It covered common remote work topics accurately, but many points felt broad and generic. This showed me that AI-generated research can be useful as a starting point, but it should be reviewed, improved, and supported with stronger academic sources before being treated as a complete research paper.

Artifact 3 · Research Report

Machine Learning vs. Deep Learning: Truck Driver Churn Prediction and Facial Recognition

This artifact is a research-style report that explains the difference between machine learning and deep learning using two real-world examples: truck driver churn prediction in logistics and facial recognition in mobile devices.

Introduction: The report compares how traditional machine learning works best with structured business data, while deep learning is better suited for complex unstructured data such as images.

Objective: To explain the practical difference between machine learning and deep learning by connecting each concept to realistic use cases.

Description: The first example focuses on truck driver churn prediction in digital freight marketplaces, which connects to my work experience in logistics. The second example focuses on facial recognition, using Apple Face ID as a real-world deep learning case.

Process: I researched machine learning, deep learning, freight marketplace platforms, and facial recognition technology. I then compared both examples based on data type, model suitability, business value, and explainability.

Tools and Technologies Used: Microsoft Word, Brightspace course materials, IBM AI/ML resources, Apple Face ID documentation, Uber Freight resources, C. H. Robinson carrier resources, ChatGPT, and Claude.

Value Proposition: This artifact demonstrates my ability to explain AI/ML concepts through practical business examples instead of only using textbook definitions.

Unique Value: The logistics example makes the artifact more personal and career-relevant because it connects AI concepts to my professional background in freight and transportation.

Relevance: Understanding when to use ML versus DL is important for real-world AI decision-making because different problems require different types of models, data, and levels of explainability.

References: Apple, IBM, Indiana Wesleyan University course content, Uber Freight, and C. H. Robinson.
